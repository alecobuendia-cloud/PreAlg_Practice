Optimization:

1 Overview

Optimization problems ask you to find the largest or smallest possible value that still follows a set of rules. You could try to guess-and-check every possibility, but there’s a faster and more reliable way to think about it: direction.

For every number in the problem that you’re allowed to choose, ask yourself:

“If I make this number bigger, does the thing I’m trying to maximize (or minimize) get bigger, get smaller, or stay the same?”

Once you know the direction, you don’t need to guess anymore, you push that number as far as the rules allow, straight toward the extreme case the direction points to.

TipThe Big Idea
  • Bigger helps → push that number as high as the rules allow.
  • Bigger hurts → push that number as low as the rules allow.

1.1 Applying This to “Largest/Smallest Mode” Problems

Take a problem like: a set of six single-digit whole numbers has a unique mode, and you want the largest possible median. You have a required number that must repeat (the mode), and several other numbers you get to choose freely, as long as none of them accidentally repeats often enough to also become a mode.

Ask the direction question: for six sorted numbers, the median depends on the 3rd and 4th values. If you make one of your free numbers bigger, does the median get bigger? Yes raising a free number (as long as it’s still larger than the required repeated number) pushes the sorted list’s upper half higher. Direction: bigger helps. So you push every free number as high as it’s allowed to go, and just as importantly you place the required repeated number as low as possible, out of the median’s way.

NoteConcept Check

Two numbers add up to \(20\). Compare \(9 \times 11\) to \(2 \times 18\). As two numbers with a fixed sum get closer together, does their product get bigger or smaller?

TipShow Answer

Bigger. \(9 \times 11 = 99\), but \(2 \times 18 = 36\). When two numbers have a fixed sum, their product is largest when the numbers are as close together as possible, and smallest when they’re as far apart as possible. Keep this pattern in mind, it shows up again below with perimeter!

1.2 Applying This to “Largest/Smallest Perimeter” Problems

Now picture a rectangle with a fixed area. If you increase one side length, the other side length has to shrink to keep the same area. What happens to the perimeter (the sum of all four sides) as you do this?

Direction: as the two side lengths move apart from each other, the perimeter grows. As they move closer together — toward being equal, i.e., a square — the perimeter shrinks. This is the exact same pattern from the Concept Check above, just applied to a sum (perimeter) instead of a product.

  • Minimum perimeter → make the shape as close to a square as possible.
  • Maximum perimeter → stretch it out as long and thin as possible (or, for blocky grid shapes, make it as zig-zaggy as possible so squares barely touch each other).

2 Practice Questions

  1. A set of six single-digit whole numbers has a unique mode of \(3\) (meaning \(3\) appears more often than any other number). What is the largest possible value of the median of these numbers?

  2. A set of five single-digit whole numbers has a unique mode of \(7\). What is the smallest possible value of the median of these numbers?

  3. A shape is built by shading unit squares on a grid so that its total area is \(12\) square units. What is the smallest possible perimeter for such a shape?

  4. Using the same rules as Question 3 (unit squares on a grid, total area of \(12\)), what is the largest possible perimeter for such a shape? (The shape must still be a single connected piece — no floating separate parts — but it’s allowed to zig-zag.)

  5. Five different whole numbers have a sum of \(50\). What is the largest possible value of the smallest number in the set?

  6. Six friends want the mode of their homework-minutes to be exactly \(45\), appearing exactly twice, using only whole-number minutes between \(20\) and \(60\). No other number is allowed to repeat. What is the smallest possible sum of all six times?

  7. A rectangular garden has a fixed area of \(48\) square meters. Here are the whole-number side-length pairs that give that area: \(1\times48\), \(2\times24\), \(3\times16\), \(4\times12\), and \(6\times8\). Find the perimeter for each pair, and confirm that the perimeter shrinks as the two side lengths get closer together. Which pair gives the smallest perimeter?

  8. True or False: If two numbers have a fixed sum, moving them further apart makes their product bigger.
