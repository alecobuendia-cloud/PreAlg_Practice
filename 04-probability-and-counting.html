Probability and Counting

1 Overview

Probability measures how likely something is to happen, and it’s always a fraction: the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of possible outcomes. \[P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{favorable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}\] To use this correctly, you need to count carefully and stay organized: for something like rolling two dice, it helps to write out a table or list of every possible combination rather than trying to count in your head!

NoteConcept Check

You roll two dice and want the probability the sum is \(7\). Before counting anything, how many total possible outcomes are there when two dice are rolled?

TipShow Answer

\(36\) total outcomes — there are \(6\) possibilities for the first die and \(6\) for the second, so \(6 \times 6 = 36\).

Some events are independent, which means the outcome of one event has no effect on the outcome of the next. Flipping a coin twice, rolling dice, or shooting free throws (when told the shooting percentage stays the same) are all independent events. When you want the probability that two independent events both happen, you multiply their individual probabilities together: \[P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)\]

ImportantCommon Mistake

If a problem says an event happens with some probability “each time” (like a free-throw percentage), don’t just report that single probability as your final answer when the question asks about two or more events in a row. You need to multiply the probability by itself once for every event.

Other events are dependent, which usually happens when you’re removing items from a group without putting them back — like pulling two candies from a jar one at a time. In this case, the second event’s probability depends on what happened in the first draw, because the total number of items (and possibly the number of favorable items) has changed: \[P(A \text{ then } B) = P(A) \times P(B \mid A \text{ happened})\] You still multiply the two probabilities together, but the second fraction will look different from the first because the group has shrunk.

NoteConcept Check

A bag has marbles. Which situation below is dependent, and which is independent? (a) Drawing a marble, recording its color, and putting it back before drawing again. (b) Drawing a marble and keeping it out, then drawing a second one.

TipShow Answer
  1. is independent — putting the marble back means the bag is exactly the same for the second draw. (b) is dependent — removing the marble changes both the total count and possibly the number of favorable outcomes for the second draw.

Finally, counting problems ask “how many different ways can this happen?” For simple situations, the counting principle says you multiply the number of choices at each step together. For arranging a group of different items in order (like lining people up for a photo), you multiply by one fewer each time, this is called a permutation: for \(n\) items, there are \(n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times 1\) ways to arrange them, written as \(n!\) (“\(n\) factorial”).

2 Practice Questions

  1. A bag has \(4\) red marbles, \(5\) blue marbles, and \(3\) green marbles. If one marble is drawn at random, what is the probability it is blue?

  2. Two coins are flipped. List all the possible outcomes, then find the probability that both coins land on heads.

  3. Two dice are rolled. What is the probability that the sum of the two dice is exactly \(8\)?

  4. Two dice are rolled. What is the probability that the sum of the two dice is greater than \(9\)?

  5. Maria makes \(70\%\) of her free throws. What is the probability that she makes her next two free throws in a row?

  6. A spinner lands on red with probability \(\dfrac{1}{3}\). If Jordan spins the spinner twice, what is the probability that it does not land on red either time?

  7. A weighted coin lands on heads \(80\%\) of the time. If it is flipped three times, what is the probability that all three flips are heads?

  8. A bag contains \(3\) black balls and \(6\) white balls. If two balls are drawn from the bag one at a time, without putting the first one back, what is the probability that both balls are white?

  9. A jar has \(5\) lemon candies and \(3\) cherry candies. Two candies are picked at random, one after another, without replacement. What is the probability that both candies are lemon?

  10. Using the same jar from Question 9 (\(5\) lemon, \(3\) cherry), what is the probability that the first candy picked is lemon and the second candy picked is cherry?

  11. How many different ways can \(4\) students (Ann, Ben, Cara, and Dan) be lined up in a row for a photo?

  12. A sandwich shop lets you choose \(1\) type of bread (\(3\) choices), \(1\) type of filling (\(3\) choices), and \(1\) type of cheese (\(2\) choices). How many different sandwiches are possible?
